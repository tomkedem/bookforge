In unsupervised learning, we work with data that has no pre-labeled tags. There is no known correct answer, and no one directing the model what to look for.

Instead of learning from examples with answers, the model tries to understand the structure of the data itself. It searches for patterns, relationships, and similarities between different points.

The diagram shows there is no marking of categories. The data comes as is, and the model must discover by itself how they divide or relate to each other.

The goal here is not to predict a known result, but to discover hidden structure. For example, identifying similar groups of items or customers, or finding relationships between phenomena.

The model is based on trends and commonalities between the data, and through them creates a new division or representation of them.

In unsupervised learning, the model receives data without labels. There is no target variable, and no correct answer guiding the learning.

Instead of learning "what's correct", the model tries to understand "what's similar to what". It searches for relationships, patterns, and structures within the data itself.

The process starts with a collection of data as is. The algorithm goes through the data and tries to identify groups, relationships, or structures that repeat. For example, it can identify that certain items are similar to each other in color, shape, or behavior.

The diagram shows that initially the data is not divided. After running the algorithm, they organize into groups. This is a process of pattern recognition, where the model creates order from unlabeled information.

The result is not a prediction of a value, but a division or new representation of the data. This allows better understanding of their internal structure, even without prior information.
