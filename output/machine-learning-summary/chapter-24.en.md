# Deep Learning

Deep Learning is a natural continuation of neural networks, but with one central difference: depth.

Instead of a network with one or two layers, here we're talking about networks with a large number of computational layers. Each layer learns a different representation of the data, and the deeper you go in the network, the more abstract and complex the representations become.

When data enters the network, it passes through many layers. Each layer performs additional processing and emphasizes certain patterns. The result is that the network is capable of learning very complex relationships that cannot be identified using simpler models.

This is the idea seen in the diagram: not just a transition from input to output, but a chain of stages in which the information is "reshaped" again and again.

Deep Learning is a subfield of Machine Learning. The meaning is that it's not a separate approach, but an expansion of the same method, when using deeper networks and a larger amount of data.

For such networks to work well, a combination of three things is required: lots of data, high computing power, and advanced training methods. Therefore this field has developed a lot in recent years with the increase in computing power and information availability.

These models don't "understand" the world like humans, but they manage to identify very complex patterns, and sometimes even approach human performance in certain tasks.

The uses of Deep Learning are very broad. It stands behind fields such as natural language processing, speech recognition, image recognition, autonomous driving systems, and also Generative AI systems that create text, images, and code.
